Healthcare UX Accessibility (WCAG) Prototyping

The Need

Designing for healthcare is a high-stakes environment where efficiency can literally impact lives. A leading hospital network was struggling with a clunky, outdated patient management system that was causing significant administrative overhead. The goal was to build a new platform from the ground up, with a primary focus on reducing cognitive load for medical staff and ensuring full accessibility compliance with WCAG 2.1 AA standards.

My Role

I led the UX and UI design, with a special focus on accessibility. My process was rooted in deep empathy, involving contextual inquiry, task analysis, and iterative prototyping and testing with medical professionals.

The Process

Discovery & Research: I spent two weeks shadowing nurses and doctors on their rounds. This firsthand observation was invaluable. I identified major pain points, such as excessive clicking to access patient vitals and difficulty reading text in poorly lit rooms.

Design & Prototyping: Using Figma, I created high-fidelity, interactive prototypes. Every design choice, from color palettes to font sizes, was vetted against WCAG 2.1 AA guidelines. The design featured a dark mode to reduce eye strain during night shifts, a feature specifically requested by nurses.

Testing & Iteration: We conducted usability tests with 10 medical professionals in a simulated environment. The tests confirmed that the task-based approach was much faster, but revealed that some icons were not immediately intuitive. I iterated on the icon library, adding text labels for clarity, which tested perfectly in the second round.

The Result & Impact

The new system was a resounding success. We measurably reduced administrative time by 50%, freeing up hundreds of hours per week across the hospital network. This wasn't just a win for efficiency; it meant that medical staff could dedicate more of their valuable time to what truly matters: patient care.
